Overview

Given a function $ f: ^n $ that is sufficiently smooth (i.e., has continuous partial derivatives up to the desired order) around a point $ = (a_1, a_2, , a_n) $, the multivariate Taylor expansion of $ f $ around $ $ up to order $ k $ is given by:

\[ f(\mathbf{x}) = f(\mathbf{a}) + \sum_{|\alpha| = 1} \frac{D^\alpha f(\mathbf{a})}{\alpha!} (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a})^\alpha + \sum_{|\alpha| = 2} \frac{D^\alpha f(\mathbf{a})}{\alpha!} (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a})^\alpha + \cdots + \sum_{|\alpha| = k} \frac{D^\alpha f(\mathbf{a})}{\alpha!} (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a})^\alpha + R_k(\mathbf{x}), \]

where:

  • $ = (x_1, x_2, , x_n) $ is the point at which you're evaluating the expansion.
  • $ = (_1, _2, , _n) $ is a multi-index with non-negative integer components.
  • $ || = _1 + _2 + + _n $ is the order of the multi-index.
  • $ D^f = $ is the mixed partial derivative corresponding to $ $.
  • $ ! = _1! _2! _n! $
  • $ ( - )^= (x_1 - a_1)^{_1} (x_2 - a_2)^{_2} (x_n - a_n)^{_n} $
  • $ R_k() $ is the remainder term of order $ k $.

Simplified Notation

For simplicity, the expansion can also be written using summations over the total order:

\[ f(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{|\alpha| \leq k} \frac{D^\alpha f(\mathbf{a})}{\alpha!} (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a})^\alpha + R_k(\mathbf{x}). \]

Example: Two Variables

Let's consider a function $ f(x, y) $ of two variables and expand it around the point $ (a, b) $.

First-Order (Linear) Expansion

\[ f(x, y) \approx f(a, b) + f_x(a, b)(x - a) + f_y(a, b)(y - b) \]

where: - $ f_x $ and $ f_y $ are the first partial derivatives with respect to $ x $ and $ y $, respectively.

Second-Order Expansion

\[ \begin{aligned} f(x, y) \approx & f(a, b) + f_x(a, b)(x - a) + f_y(a, b)(y - b) \\ & + \frac{1}{2} f_{xx}(a, b)(x - a)^2 + f_{xy}(a, b)(x - a)(y - b) + \frac{1}{2} f_{yy}(a, b)(y - b)^2 \\ & + \text{Higher-order terms} + R_2(x, y) \end{aligned} \]

Here, $ f_{xx} $, $ f_{xy} $, and $ f_{yy} $ are the second-order partial derivatives.

General Formula Using Multi-Indices

For a clearer understanding, here's how multi-indices work in the general case.

Multi-Index Definition

A multi-index $ = (_1, _2, , _n) $ simplifies the notation for partial derivatives and polynomial terms. For example, in three variables:

  • $ = (2, 0, 1) $ corresponds to the second derivative with respect to $ x_1 $ twice and once with respect to $ x_3 $, i.e., $ $.

Taylor Series up to Order $ k $

\[ f(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{|\alpha| \leq k} \frac{D^\alpha f(\mathbf{a})}{\alpha!} (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a})^\alpha + R_k(\mathbf{x}) \]

Remainder Term

The remainder term $ R_k() $ captures the error between the actual function and its Taylor polynomial up to order $ k $. One common form of the remainder (the integral form) in multiple dimensions is:

\[ R_k(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{|\alpha| = k+1} \frac{D^\alpha f(\mathbf{c})}{\alpha!} (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a})^\alpha \]

Applications

Multivariate Taylor expansions are widely used in various fields, including:

  • Optimization: Approximating objective functions to find local minima or maxima.
  • Numerical Analysis: Developing numerical methods for solving partial differential equations.
  • Economics: Modeling functions with multiple variables to study changes in markets.
  • Engineering: Analyzing systems with multiple interacting components.

Example: Second-Order Taylor Expansion in Two Variables

Let's compute the second-order Taylor expansion of $ f(x, y) = e^{x + y} $ around the point $ (0, 0) $.

Step 1: Compute Partial Derivatives

\[ \begin{aligned} f(x, y) &= e^{x + y} \\ f(0, 0) &= 1 \\ f_x &= e^{x + y} \quad \Rightarrow \quad f_x(0, 0) = 1 \\ f_y &= e^{x + y} \quad \Rightarrow \quad f_y(0, 0) = 1 \\ f_{xx} &= e^{x + y} \quad \Rightarrow \quad f_{xx}(0, 0) = 1 \\ f_{xy} &= e^{x + y} \quad \Rightarrow \quad f_{xy}(0, 0) = 1 \\ f_{yy} &= e^{x + y} \quad \Rightarrow \quad f_{yy}(0, 0) = 1 \\ \end{aligned} \]

Step 2: Construct the Taylor Polynomial

\[ \begin{aligned} f(x, y) \approx & 1 + (1)(x) + (1)(y) \\ & + \frac{1}{2}(1)x^2 + (1)xy + \frac{1}{2}(1)y^2 \\ = & 1 + x + y + \frac{1}{2}x^2 + xy + \frac{1}{2}y^2 \end{aligned} \]

Step 3: Interpretation

This polynomial approximates $ e^{x + y} $ near $ (0, 0) $ up to second-order terms. The accuracy of the approximation improves as $ (x, y) $ gets closer to $ (0, 0) $.

Conclusion

The multivariate Taylor expansion is a powerful tool for approximating functions of several variables near a specific point. By incorporating partial derivatives of various orders, it captures the local behavior of the function, enabling applications across mathematics, science, and engineering.
